Curating Personalized NYC Parks Experiences

A Redesign of the NYC Parks Website

The NYC Parks website (nycgovparks.org) is a hub of information and resources for locals and tourists alike. However, the site is due for a redesign because of accessibility, compatibility, and navigation issues. It is also lacking in aesthetics and displays an overwhelming amount of information at once. Through a series of research and design methodologies, desktop and mobile versions of an updated NYC Parks website were created with improved flexibility and aesthetics in mind.

When: September - December 2022

Team: Mary Haws (me), Radhika Mehra, Josie Xu, Yiying Zhou

My Role: writing survey questions, conducting interviews, developing a card sort and tree test, competitive analysis, sketching, wireframing, prototyping, conducting user tests

The Challenge

Redesign the NYC Parks website for desktop and mobile to improve its user experience, considering its accessibility, responsiveness, navigation, and aesthetics.

We audited the current website to better understand its room for improvement

Our evaluation revealed problems with accessibility, responsiveness, navigation, and aesthetics. With vast amounts of information for millions of park visitors, we recognized how crucial it is for the website to provide a usable and enjoyable experience.

Our process included research, definition, exploration, and implementation

Research

Generative research highlighted the need to cater to park visitors of all ages on the website

We conducted 12 semi-structured interviews and used the 5 As (actors, atmospheres, attitudes, artifacts, and activities) framework to uncover insights.

A pattern emerged: different age groups/life stages corresponded with particular activities, interests, preferences, and needs.

Our target users were characterized into three groups based on their life stage and goals for visiting NYC parks

Three personas with corresponding journey maps were created to reflect the three major groups we interviewed: young adults, parents, and seniors.

Definition

Research revealed a common motivation among our three user groups for visiting parks in NYC

All of our participants and user groups expressed the need for active recreation in the parks, which is reflected in our problem statement.

The Problem

How might we help people interested in active recreation at NYC’s parks find suitable activities for their unique needs and interests?

Exploration

Further research helped us build an information architecture that could meet the varied needs of park visitors

First, we conducted a card sorting study to inform the site structure

We audited the current NYC Parks website to create cards that would capture its key content. Our open card sorting study with 86 cards and 22 participants revealed that the level of specificity with which participant grouped cards varied: some were highly specific groups like “water sports,” while some were more general like “outdoor activities.” About 1/3 of our participants decided to group the cards by personal preference, creating “liked” and “disliked” groups.

Next, we tree tested a site map based on the results from card sorting

A site map was developed based on the most common categories from card sorting. We conducted a tree testing study with 11 participants, but 90% of the participants failed to locate information about hiking. Participants were divided on how to approach this task: some looked for parks first, while other search by activity. This behavior revealed that users will have different priorities when navigating the site, suggesting the need to consolidate options.

The final site map centered around an advanced search that would allow for a more personalized experience

Because the card sort and tree test revealed that participants varied in specificity, preferences, and priorities, we opted for a search-centric information architecture. The advanced search feature would be the site's core functionality for getting users where they need to go more quickly, and would be immediately accessible from the homepage.

A competitive review helped us put our search-centric architecture into focus: understanding successes and failures on other websites

We learned that search functions were most successful with a wide range of filters that can help guide users through their options.

Implementation

We designed solutions that would support users' unique goals, testing to ensure their accessibility, responsivity, ease of navigation, and aesthetics

Sketching and wireframing helped us visualize key flows, elements, and layouts, including advanced search and user profiles

We experimented with both desktop and mobile layouts at this stage. In addition to a core advanced search aimed at creating a flexible experience for users, we included the ability to create user profiles with NYC parks in our designs. This would contribute to a personalized experience, as users could quickly access their favorite activities.

Testing revealed a need for improved clarity as users curated content based on their personal goals and interests

11 participants completed moderated user tests with our wireframes. They were given the following tasks:

  • Find tennis courts in Manhattan for your team practice

  • Log in to your account and view your saved activities

Finding #1: Participants had trouble remembering their selections when using the search filter drop-downs

We improved the advanced search filters by adding a row of filter chips that display a user's current selections. What seems like an obvious finding in retrospect would have been overlooked without user testing.

Finding #2: Navigation for the account pages confused participants, particularly “My Collection” for saved activities

We renamed “My Collection” to “Favorites” to clarify the content of this key profile feature.

Our final prototype integrates these findings with an inviting aesthetic for a fresh experience of the NYC Parks website

We aimed for an aesthetic that would feel modern yet timeless, evoking the feeling of being surrounded by nature.

The prototype includes the following flows:

  • Find tennis courts in Manhattan for your team practice

  • Log in to your account and view your saved activities

Reflection & Next Steps

This project helped me gain valuable experience in capturing the complexity of user needs and use cases. Because we conducted interviews, a card sorting study, a tree test, and usability tests, I saw how powerful the synthesis of data from multiple sources is in driving a design forward. As a result, I believe our final prototype demonstrates a foundation in user research as well as a commitment to an aesthetic experience.

The next steps for this project would include validating our final prototype with more user testing; this would allow for further refinement of our flows and interface. We can also explore the workflows and potential designs for the other branches of the site (“Get Involved” for volunteering and “About NYC Parks” for park history) since we mainly focused on park visitors using the “Plan Your Visit” branch for advanced searches.
